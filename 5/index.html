Intro

In this project, I attempt to experiment with diffusion models to generate a variety of images (Part A) and train a flow matching model on the MNIST dataset (Part B).

Part A: 0 and 1.1
Text Prompts + Implementing the Forward Process

In this part, I came up with some text prompts and generated their embeddings. Here are some of my prompts: 'an oil painting of a snowy mountain village', 'a photo of a Santorini beach', 'a photo of an old man', 'a photo of a shiba', 'a lithograph of waterfalls', 'a lithograph of a skull', 'an oil painting of a campfire with smoke', 'an oil painting of a waterfall coming out of a cave', 'a portrait of a bearded old man', 'a portrait of a rabbit', 'a photo of a blue human eye', 'a photo of a spiral galaxy in space', 'a photo of a full moon', 'close-up of a tiger‚Äôs face, straight on, filling the frame', and 'close-up of an orange tabby cat‚Äôs face, straight on, slightly blurred'. I used random seed 26 throughout my code. Below, I'll present the initial images generated for three of my prompts at two different num_inference_steps. We have "a portrait of a rabbit", "a view inside a deep dark cave", and "a photo of a blue human eye" at both 20 steps and 100 steps.

We can see that increasing the number of inference steps generally improved detail across the three prompts. With 20 steps, the images captured the general idea, but some parts look blurry or stylized in unintended ways. The rabbit has some exaggerated colors and the blue eye has strange artifacts. At 100 steps, the outputs became more clear and shapes were better defined, however, there were still some issues. In fact, for some of the prompts having more steps seemed to make the generated image worse since it felt "hyper-realistic" such as for the rabbit. This led me to realize that we likely want a different number of inference steps based on the prompt and its complexity.

Part A: 1.3
One-Step Denoising

In this part, I attempt to denoise the campanile images by estimating the noise using stage_1.unet and subtracting it with the formula: (Noisy Image - sqrt(1-alpha) * Noise) / sqrt(alpha). Below, you can see the original, noisy and denoised images for the three noise levels. We can see that the results are much better across all three noise levels but still deteriorate at the higher levels.

Part A: 1.4
