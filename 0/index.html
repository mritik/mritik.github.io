Perspective, Compression & the Dolly Zoom

In this warm-up I explored how camera distance (not just focal length) changes perspective, why longer lenses appear to “compress” space, and how to perform the classic dolly zoom. Each part below includes my method, results, and a short explanation.

Part 1
Selfie — The Wrong Way vs. The Right Way

I shot two portraits with the face roughly the same size in frame: (A) close to the camera with a short focal length, and (B) farther away with a longer focal length (zoomed-in).

Why this happens

  • Perspective is set by camera-to-subject distance. Close distance exaggerates features nearer to the lens.
  • Changing focal length only crops/expands field of view; the distance change between A and B fixes the perspective.
Part 2
Architectural Perspective Compression

I photographed a long street view twice with similar framing of the far buildings: (A) from far away while zoomed in, and (B) from closer without zoom. The zoomed-in, farther shot appears “flatter” because relative depth differences are reduced at longer distances.

Takeaway

“Compression” is a perspective effect from increased subject distance, not an intrinsic property of focal length alone.

Part 3
The Dolly Zoom

I created a dolly zoom by moving the camera backwards while zooming in so the subject stays the same size. This keeps foreground scale constant while dramatically changing the background perspective.

Procedure

  1. Pick a subject and align a central reference (e.g., eyes or an object edge).
  2. Start close, frame the subject, take a photo.
  3. Step back ~10–20% farther, zoom in to keep subject the same size, take another photo.
  4. Repeat for 6–10 frames; convert the sequence to a GIF/video.
Notes & Reflection
  • Face distortion in selfies is minimized by standing back and zooming in slightly.
  • For architecture, distance stabilizes parallel lines and reduces apparent depth exaggeration.
  • Dolly zoom = distance change (perspective) + focal length change (field of view) with constant subject size.
